How Many Castles Are in Ireland?

Ireland has over 1,620 recorded castles and fortified sites, ranging from major medieval strongholds to small rural tower houses scattered across the countryside. While many survive only as ruins or partial foundations, each site contributes to the wider picture of Ireland’s defensive and political history. This dataset is built using OpenStreetMap records supported by historical sources and manual verification, ensuring that both well‑known landmarks and lesser‑known local sites are accurately represented.

Castles Included

The castles shown on this map represent the full range of fortified sites recorded across Ireland, from major medieval strongholds to small, isolated tower houses that once anchored local communities. Many of the entries mark the remains of Anglo‑Norman keeps built to secure newly claimed territory, while others highlight later Gaelic and English tower houses that served as both residences and defensive structures during periods of political tension.

The map also includes ruined castles, partial foundations, fortified manor houses, and sites where only fragments survive, giving a complete picture of how widespread defensive architecture once was. By displaying every mapped site rather than only the well‑known landmarks, the dataset reveals the true density of Ireland’s fortified landscape and shows how deeply these structures shaped settlement patterns, regional power, and local identity over several centuries.

Origins of Castles in Ireland

Castles in Ireland trace their origins to the arrival of the Normans in the late 12th century, when fortified structures became essential for controlling territory and establishing administrative power. Early fortifications were quickly replaced by stone keeps and later by the distinctive Irish tower houses that spread across the country from the 14th to 17th centuries.

These buildings served multiple roles: military defence, symbols of authority, centres of local governance, and secure residences for influential families. Over time, both Anglo‑Norman lords and Gaelic chieftains adopted castle‑building as a way to reinforce their status and protect their lands, leaving behind a landscape marked by thousands of fortified sites.

The surviving castles reflect centuries of political tension, shifting alliances, and regional power struggles, making them one of the most tangible records of Ireland’s medieval history.
